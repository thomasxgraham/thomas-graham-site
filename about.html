UX Researcher · Product Thinker

I care about making difficult moments easier to navigate.

A lot of the problems I’m drawn to are the ones that weigh heavily on people’s lives. In healthcare especially, that means looking closely at the moments where stress is high, clarity is low, and the experience has to do more than simply function, it has to help.

Most research problems turn out to be decision problems in disguise. Teams rarely need more data — they need someone who can tell them what it means and what to do next. That’s the part I’ve always cared most about getting right.

Perspective

My perspective comes from working across the whole product life cycle.

UX research often gets siloed at one end of the product process. I’ve worked across all of it — research, design, delivery, and the operational infrastructure that makes teams repeatable. That range changes how I frame problems and what I think is worth asking.

It also means I’ve spent enough time on the build and ship side to know what makes research actually useful to the people doing the work — and what makes it land in a deck that no one reopens.

Working style

How I show up on teams

I do my best work with teams that genuinely want to know what’s true, even when the answer reshapes the plan.

I prefer being embedded with a team rather than operating at a distance. The best work happens when research, design, product, and engineering are moving together.

Working with me

You can count on me to ask the questions the team hasn't gotten around to asking yet — and to keep asking them until we know what we're actually dealing with.

Beyond work

Meaningful work matters to me on and off the job.

After the Pulse nightclub tragedy in Orlando, my team and I organized a volunteer-led T-shirt fundraiser to support victims’ families. The effort raised $80,000 in a matter of weeks. It’s not a conventional portfolio case study, but it reflects the kind of leadership I value most: moving quickly, creating something purposeful, and rallying people around a real need. Some of my best research instincts — reading a situation fast, learning to listen for what moves people, and not waiting for permission to act — came from moments like that one.
