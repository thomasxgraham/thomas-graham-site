UX Researcher · Product Thinker

I care about making difficult moments easier to navigate.

A lot of the problems I’m drawn to are the ones that weigh heavily on people’s lives. In healthcare especially, that means looking closely at the moments where stress is high, clarity is low, and the experience has to do more than simply function, it has to help.

After twenty years, I’ve learned that most research problems are really decision problems in disguise. Teams don’t need more data — they need someone who can tell them what it means and what to do next. That’s the part I’ve always cared most about getting right.

Perspective

My perspective comes from working across the whole product life cycle.

Most UX researchers are good at one end of the process. I’ve worked across all of it — research, design, delivery, and the operational infrastructure that makes teams repeatable. That range changes how I frame problems and what I think is worth asking.

I’m not interested in collecting interesting observations that go nowhere. My job isn’t done when the report is finished. It’s done when the team has what they need to move.

Working style

How I show up on teams

I do my best work with teams that care deeply about solving real problems, teams that are willing to test assumptions, peel back the layers, and put in the effort to understand what will actually help the people they’re building for.

I prefer being embedded with a team rather than operating at a distance. The best work happens when research, design, product, and engineering are moving together.

Working with me

You can count on me to ask the questions the team hasn't gotten around to asking yet — and to keep asking them until we know what we're actually dealing with.

Beyond work

Meaningful work matters to me on and off the job.

After the Pulse nightclub tragedy in Orlando, my team and I organized a volunteer-led T-shirt fundraiser to support victims’ families. The effort raised $80,000 in a matter of weeks. It’s not a conventional portfolio case study, but it reflects the kind of leadership I value most: moving quickly, creating something purposeful, and rallying people around a real need. Some of my best research instincts — reading a situation fast, knowing what will move people, and not waiting for permission — came from moments like that one.
