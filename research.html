AdventHealth · IT UX Researcher Intermediate

Reducing ED onboarding friction in a BYOD model

TL;DR

I benchmarked the MyChart Bedside ED onboarding journey to show exactly where a BYOD registration flow was creating delays for patients and pulling nurses into a support role.

When AdventHealth shifted Emergency Department bedside access from hospital tablets to patient-owned devices, it handed patients a full registration flow at the worst possible moment. I led a rapid timing study to measure the end-to-end journey and identify where the process was breaking down.

The study showed a median completion time of 5:58, isolated the app handoff as a meaningful friction point, and gave leadership the evidence needed to pursue a code-based entry model that removes redundant setup work in an acute-care context.

AdventHealth · UX Researcher

Understanding why patients call support

TL;DR

I re-coded Five9 support-call data to uncover one larger access-and-identity failure lifecycle hiding behind repeated “can’t log in” complaints.

The original call tags were too broad and inconsistent to tell a useful story, so I rebuilt the categorization system from the transcripts up. Once the noise was removed, the pattern was clear: most calls were not about different product problems. They were different expressions of the same hidden access failure.

The work reframed support demand as a product and systems problem. It showed how verification failures, wrong delivery targets, incomplete record linkage, and retries that triggered lockouts were combining into one lifecycle that the system could not explain or repair without human intervention.

AdventHealth · UX Researcher

Testing whether Connect Records clarity would reduce support calls

TL;DR

I ran a multivariate prototype study to test whether clearer codeless access would reduce support demand, and the data showed the real issue was patient mental models, not simple visibility.

The hypothesis was straightforward: if we made the “I don’t have a code” path more visible and more clearly explained, fewer patients would feel the need to call support. I tested the current flow against two redesigned alternatives across 45 participants.

The most important finding was not which variant won. It was that all three were navigable enough. The deeper issue was that patients did not understand what the code was, where it came from, or what connecting records meant for them. That pushed the work back toward the upstream access-dependency issues identified in the telephony analysis.

Prototype experimentation, hypothesis testing, mixed-method interpretation, and the willingness to let the data disprove a preferred UI explanation

AdventHealth · IT UX Researcher Intermediate

Improving new-user conversion in the AdventHealth app

TL;DR

I evaluated the onboarding experience to identify where the AdventHealth app was asking too much before users felt enough value to keep going.

Despite promising features, the AdventHealth mobile app was struggling to retain new users beyond onboarding. I reviewed the setup flow through a mix of comparative analysis, heuristic evaluation, and behavioral design thinking to surface the specific frictions reducing activation and early return usage.

The work reframed onboarding as a value-delivery problem rather than a simple registration problem. It clarified how time-to-value, motivational cues, and feature discovery could make the setup experience feel more worth finishing and more likely to support retention.

Reducing time-to-insight from two weeks to four days

TL;DR

I introduced AI-assisted analysis to replace manual spreadsheet-heavy workflows in synthesis, helping reduce time-to-insight from roughly two weeks to four days.

Ascentus · Director, UX Design

Redeveloping AddictionCenter.com for trust, search, and support

TL;DR

I led the redevelopment of a high-traffic recovery guide so people could research treatment with more trust, flexibility, and confidence.

Addiction Center was already generating calls, but the experience was too dependent on pressure, too cluttered structurally, and too weak on mobile for the people it was trying to help. I led a redevelopment effort that combined content cleanup, mobile-first thinking, audience analysis, search redesign, and visual system changes.

The goal was to make the site feel more trustworthy and more self-directed without weakening conversion. After launch, the redesigned product drove a 30% increase in conversions, an 18% increase in site traffic, higher call volume, and rising treatment-center ad sales.

Designing the E2-D training experience across courseware and simulation

TL;DR

I led the interface and visual implementation for a long-running training program, then helped reshape the delivery model so the team could actually ship it on time.

This three-year program covered the design and implementation of the courseware and PC-simulation experience for the E2-D Advanced Hawkeye training curriculum. My role combined leadership, interface design, 3D asset work, and team coordination across a large visual production effort.

The work mattered for two reasons. First, it created a durable interface system that could hold up over years of classroom use. Second, a revised waterfall-plus-agile roadmap helped the team avoid a major schedule overrun and deliver three months earlier than expected.
