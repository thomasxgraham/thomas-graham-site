Introduction

Trust in online scheduling depended on relevance, clarity, and timing from the first search onward.

This report presents the findings from a recent Discovery study aimed at understanding why patients are or are not scheduling appointments using our digital services. The study focused on uncovering insights to improve the user experience and increase the adoption of our online scheduling platform.

Participants were observed interacting with ZocDoc, a third-party platform that helps users find healthcare providers. The study involved comprehensive feedback from patients to identify friction points and successes in the process.

What emerged most clearly was that people did not need more information as much as they needed stronger signals that the system understood them. When insurance, search relevance, specialty fit, and provider details lined up, confidence rose. When those signals broke down, trust dropped quickly.

Methodology

Patients were interviewed in context as they searched for care through ZocDoc.

Participants were asked a series of questions during discovery interviews to gather their experiences and opinions on various aspects of using ZocDoc.

The study covered personal experiences searching for a primary care provider, challenges faced during the search, thoughts on the guided search experience, and preferences for features like voice input and AI assistance. The goal was to understand where trust increased, where friction surfaced, and what would make online scheduling feel clearer, faster, and more useful.

Key findings

The clearest signals clustered around trust, relevance, and the amount of work search demanded.

Participants appreciate features like ensuring the system knows about their insurance information upfront and before shopping, the variety of doctor options, and modern design but have concerns about search accuracy, sorting functionality, and the guided search process.

Finding 01

Trust was built early, and it broke early.

The inclusion of insurance information boosts confidence in coverage and aids in selecting providers. Participants also appreciated the straightforwardness of the search page when it focused on essential elements like insurance, location, and doctor type.

That confidence was fragile. When irrelevant providers were included in the results, especially when someone searched for a specialist and saw unrelated providers embedded within the results, trust in the service quickly eroded and participants described the experience as something they might abandon.

Finding 02

Search quality mattered as much as choice.

Participants found sorting functionality unclear and disorganized. They struggled with ordering by location, had trouble understanding titles and the mix of practices versus individuals, and wanted doctor specialties to be more prominently displayed.

The issue was not a lack of options. It was whether the results felt coherent. When people asked for a specialist and saw primary care doctors or results outside the specified area, the experience stopped feeling dependable.

Finding 03

People used reviews to judge fit, but availability often decided the choice.

Participants preferred detailed reviews for a comprehensive understanding, valued information like network status and board certifications, and wanted the age of reviews to be visible because recent negative reviews may indicate a decline in quality.

At the same time, appointment availability often outweighed high ratings. The platform felt most useful when it helped people quickly understand both credibility and practical availability.

Finding 04

Guidance was helpful when it narrowed the search, not when it interrupted it.

Participants appreciated preliminary questions that could narrow results and save time, especially when they were looking for specialists and needed more specificity. They valued the ability to move from a broad request into something more precise.

But the guided process also felt risky when it arrived too late or behaved like a roadblock. Several participants wanted the additional questions earlier in the flow, while others wanted direct access to all providers for their insurance without unnecessary steps.

Finding 05

AI and voice were acceptable as optional convenience, not as the core path.

Participants were open to AI-generated summaries and voice input, especially on mobile, and some felt those features made the platform feel modern and innovative.

They were equally clear about the limits. Concerns about control, permissions, privacy, and bias meant that typing remained the preferred default, especially on desktop. Flexibility mattered more than novelty.

Other parts of the report reinforced the same pattern. Participants expressed skepticism about sponsored listings, but not outright rejection. They accepted the need for revenue when listings were clearly marked and still relevant to their search. What they resisted was the sense that sponsorship might override search quality.

Participants were also open to alternative options and non-specialists when those choices felt appropriate and transparent. Even cross-selling could work if it respected the reason someone came to the platform in the first place. Across these topics, the same idea kept resurfacing: the experience worked best when it reduced uncertainty instead of adding more interpretation work to the patient.

Other observations

The guided scheduling table pointed to a few clearer themes.

The original report closes with participant-by-participant task notes. Rather than repeating that table here, this section pulls out the recurring patterns that were most useful across participants and tasks.

Theme 01

Specificity helped when people were unsure how to describe what they needed.

  • Participants responded positively when the guided flow helped them move from a broad need into a more specific search.
  • This was especially useful in cases where someone knew the general category but not the exact provider type they should be looking for.
  • When the narrowing felt accurate, the guided step made the search feel more focused rather than more complicated.
“I really like this screen. If I didn’t know how to describe my condition, I like that they’re giving me options to work with.”
“I think it's great. I'm really doing a very specific search. From there, I can see specialists that can take care of me based on my needs.”

What this suggests: Guided specificity is valuable when it helps people clarify a need they cannot easily name on their own.

Theme 02

People wanted more control over how they described symptoms, conditions, and preferences.

  • Multiple participants interpreted the interface as something that could support more than one choice.
  • They wanted the ability to express overlapping conditions, not just select a single issue at a time.
  • There were also signals that pre-set preferences or connected health records could reduce repetitive input and make the flow feel more personalized.
“I like it. It's more specific. I'm wondering, can I pick more than one?”
“I would say the more preliminary questions, the easier this would be.”

What this suggests: The flow becomes more useful when it gives people flexible ways to describe themselves rather than forcing them into a single-path input model.

Theme 03

The guided step created friction when it felt too general, too late, or disconnected from the specialty context.

  • Participants searching in specialty categories were more likely to notice when the options felt too broad or arrived later than expected.
  • In those moments, the flow stopped feeling like helpful guidance and started feeling like an interruption.
  • There was also concern that broad option sets could still lead to too many types of doctors appearing in the results.
“Depending on what kind of pain I have, they may be able to recommend a lot of different doctors, which I'm kind of worried about. I just want one that specializes in the pain areas I have.”
“It makes sense for an orthopedic because they all have different specialties. It's just not where I expected it.”

What this suggests: Guidance works best when it preserves context, especially for specialist searches, and appears at the moment it can genuinely reduce ambiguity.

Theme 04

People were open to extra questions when those questions clearly saved time.

  • Some participants saw the guided step as a useful filter that could spare them from sorting through results on their own.
  • That benefit depended on the system producing visibly better results in return for the extra effort.
  • If the additional questions did not noticeably improve the search, confidence in the step dropped fast.
“Additional questions here are gonna save me time in filtering results.”
“I'm hoping this gets me more customized results, but after my last experience, I don't really know.”

What this suggests: The guided flow needs to earn its place by showing a clear payoff in relevance, speed, or confidence.

Conclusion

The experience worked best when it reduced uncertainty before asking patients to do more work.

The findings from this Discovery study suggest that better scheduling does not begin with adding more features. It begins with helping people feel that the system understands who they are, what kind of care they need, and why the results in front of them make sense.

Participants appreciated modern design, profile access, insurance visibility, and the variety of doctor options. Their confidence dropped when search accuracy faltered, sorting felt disorganized, or guided steps appeared too late in the process. The strongest opportunities were not ornamental. They were structural: make insurance and specialty fit visible earlier, improve result relevance, and keep guidance lightweight and optional.

Practical takeaways
  • Make insurance compatibility visible before the patient begins the search.
  • Improve specialty relevance, sorting clarity, and location logic.
  • Use guided questions to increase specificity, but introduce them earlier and only when they add value.
  • Keep AI and voice flexible and optional rather than treating them as the default path.
